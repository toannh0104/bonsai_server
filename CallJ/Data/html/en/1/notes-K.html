Key Forms:

The following examples show the key forms to be practised in this lesson.

1Objects vs People:
これはリンゴです"This is an apple" (object)
ジョンさんはいしゃです"John is a doctor" (person)
2Question vs Statement:
これはオレンジです"This is an orange?"
それはオレンジですか"Is that an orange?"
3Positive vs Negative:
サリーサンはイギリスじんです"Sally is English"
いまにしさんはイギリスじんではありません"Imanishi is not English"

The sentence you create in this lesson should all be similar to the forms shown above. The grammar required to form these sentences is covered in the text below.



Grammar Point 1: Use of the -です to explain who or what someone/something is.

です can be tranlated as "is, are, am" etc. Desu acts like a verb, in that it's form changes depending on the tense, politeness, and
sign of the sentence. In this lesson we will only be dealing with the polite, non-past form of Desu. However, the following table shows
the various forms of desu, and whilst this information is not required for this lesson, it may be usefull to become familiar with it now.

Type Plain Polite
Present, Positive だ です
Present, Negative ではない ではありません
Past, Positive だった でした
Past, Negative ではなかった ではありませんでした


Having seen the form that "です" takes, we will now consider it's use and positioning in a sentence. Consider the following examples:

"わたしはジョンです" "I am John (my name is John)"
"これはけいたいです" "This is a mobile phone"
"それはりんごではありません" "That is not an apple"
"それはバナナですか？" "Is that a banana?"

Notes:
- Desu, in whatever form, is used at the end of the sentence.
- In the above sentences, は is used as the topic marker, ie. the object that we are talking about.
- それ and これ are both pronouns, meaning "That" and "This" respecitively. See the next section for more information.
- In the last example, we can see that to form a question we simply add か to the end of the sentence.



Grammar Point 2: は and も

は is used to mark the subject of a sentence, and follows directly after the subject. For example:

これはリンゴです - "This is an apple"

In this case, "This" is the subject of the sentence.


も is used in the sense of "also", and may also be placed after the subject, replacing は. For example:

これもリンゴです - "This is also an apple"

In this case, "This" is still the subject of the sentence, but the meaning now implies "also", as in that there is another object that the subject is being equated to.



Grammar Point 3: Demonstratives

Demonstatives are the words such as "This", "that", etc. For this lesson, we will be considering the
following 4 words:　これ、それ、あれ and どれ. Which word is to be use depends on the location of the
object being referred to by the word with regards to the speaker and the listener. This is shown in the
following table (S=Speaker, L=Listener):

Japanese English Object Location
これ This Nearer S than L
それ That Nearer L than S
あれ That (over there) Away from both L & S

どれ is a little bit different as it is a question word which could be translated as "which". There are other constructs
which follow the same pattern as the above (この, その, あの; こちら, そちら, あちら etc), but these will be covered in
a later lesson.
