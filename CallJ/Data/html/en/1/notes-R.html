Key Forms:

The following examples show the key forms to be practised in this lesson.

1Objects vs People:
Kore ha ringo desu"This is an apple" (object)
jyonsan ha isha desu"John is a doctor" (person)
2Question vs Statement:
Kore ha orenji desu"This is an orange?"
Sore ha orenji desu ka"Is that an orange?"
3Positive vs Negative:
sari-san ha igirisujin desu"Sally is English"
Imanishi san ha igirisujinde ha arimasen"Imanishi is not English"

The sentence you create in this lesson should all be similar to the forms shown above. The grammar required to form these sentences is covered in the text below.



Grammar Point 1: Use of the -desu to explain who or what someone/something is.

Desu can be tranlated as "is, are, am" etc. Desu acts like a verb, in that it's form changes depending on the tense, politeness, and
sign of the sentence. In this lesson we will only be dealing with the polite, non-past form of Desu. However, the following table shows
the various forms of desu, and whilst this information is not required for this lesson, it may be usefull to become familiar with it now.

Type Plain Polite
Present, Positive Da Desu (です)
Present, Negative De wa nai De wa arimasen (ではありません)
Past, Positive Datta Deshita
Past, Negative De wa nakatta De wa arimasen deshita


Having seen the form that "desu" takes, we will now consider it's use and positioning in a sentence. Consider the following examples:

"Watashi ha John desu" "I am John (my name is John)"
"Kore ha keitai desu" "This is a mobile phone"
"Sore ha ringo de ha arimasen" "That is not an apple"
"Sore ha banana desu ka" "Is that a banana?"

Notes:
- Desu, in whatever form, is used at the end of the sentence.
- In the above sentences, "ha" is used as the topic marker, ie. the object that we are talking about.
- Sore and Kore are both pronouns, meaning "That" and "This" respecitively. See the next section for more information.
- In the last example, we can see that to form a question we simply add ka? to the end of the sentence.



Grammar Point 2: "ha" and "mo"

"Ha" (pronounced wa) is used to mark the subject of a sentence, and follows directly after the subject. For example:

Kore ha ringo desu - "This is an apple"

In this case, "This"(kore) is the subject of the sentence.


"Mo" is used in the sense of "also", and may also be placed after the subject, replacing "Ha". For example:

Kore mo ringo desu - "This is also an apple"

In this case, "This" is still the subject of the sentence, but the meaning now implies "also", as in that there is another object that the subject is being equated to.



Grammar Point 3: Demonstratives

Demonstatives are the words such as "This", "that", etc. For this lesson, we will be considering the
following 4 words:　kore、sore、are and dore. Which word is to be use depends on the location of the
object being referred to by the word with regards to the speaker and the listener. This is shown in the
following table (S=Speaker, L=Listener):

Japanese English Object Location
Kore This Nearer S than L
Sore That Nearer L　than S
Are That (over there) Away from both L & S

Dore is a little bit different as it is a question word which could be translated as "which". There are other constructs
which follow the same pattern as the above (kono, sono, ano; kochira, sochira, achira etc), but these will be covered in
a later lesson.
