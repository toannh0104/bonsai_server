Grammar Point 1: Conditional assumptions with "nara"

When the word "nara" is used to mark a conditional statement, the emphasis of the sentence is on the speaker's belief in the truth of the condition, and you can think of it as giving the necessary conditions for the main sentence to be true. In other words, the emphasis is on the speaker's assumption. The word "nara" must be directly preceded by a noun, or a verb. Verbs are optionally followed by the noun marker "no".

Examples:

–¾“ú‚È‚çŠÔ‚ª‚ ‚è‚Ü‚· - "If it's tomorrow, I'm free."

‚İ‚ñ‚È‚ªs‚­‚Ì‚È‚ç„‚às‚«‚Ü‚· - "If everyone goes, I will go too."

In the first example, the speaker implies that tomorrow is the only day that he is free. Similarly, in the second sentence the speaker implies that everyone going is a condition for him, and he will not go under other circumstances.

Grammar Point 3: Natural consequences with "to"

When "to" directly follows the dictionary form of a verb, it marks a conditional clause. In this case, the conclusion or resulting state indicated by the main clause is an uncontrollable result. In other words, it must be a natural conclusion or certain fact (or one that the speaker is convinced is true), and cannot be a suggestion or expression of the speaker's volition. Because of this restriction, "to" cannot be used for giving suggestions or expressing the speaker's volition.

Grammar Point 4: General conditionals with "ba"

Finally, we will consider a way to make general conditional statements. The "ba" form of a verb is used to indicate a simple conditional statement followed by a result, with no special implications. It lacks the restriction to uncontrollable outcomes implied by "to", the completion of the condition and strict ordering implied by "tara", and the focus on the speaker's belief in the condition that "nara" gives. It is the most general way to indicate a hypothetical situation, and gives the sentence an objective nuance.
