Key Forms:

The following examples show the key forms to be practised in this lesson.

1Statement vs Question:
watashi ha terebi wo mimasu"I will watch TV."(Statement)
terebi wo mimasuka"Will you watch TV?"(Question)
2Positive vs Negative:
suzukisan ha kimasu"Suzuki will come."(Positive)
suzukisan ha kimasen"Suzuki will not come."(Negative)
3Non-Past vs Past:
watashi ha kyou toukyou he ikimasu"I will go to Tokyo today."(Non-past)
watashi ha kinou toukyou he ikimashita"I went to Tokyo yesterday."(Past)
4Polite vs Plain:
watashi ha tegami wo kakimasu"I will write a letter."(Polite)
watashi ha tegami wo kaku"I will write a letter."(Plain)
watashi ha tegami wo kakimashita"I wrote a letter."(Polite)
watashi ha tegami wo kaita"I wrote a letter."(Plain)

The sentences you create in this lesson should all be similar to the forms shown above. The grammar required to form these sentences is covered in the text below.



Grammar Point 1: Verbs

There are 3 groups of verbs, and the manner in which a verb changes form depends on which group it is in. There are numerous group 1 and group 2 verbs, and within each group the rules for expanding the verbs may be easily defined. Group 3 consists of those few verbs that do not follow the rules so closely, and are dealt with separately.

In this lesson we will be using verbs in the polite and plain (dictionary) form, and will be considering the verb in both positive and negative forms. We will also be using the verb in its past and non-past forms. Thus, by considering the combinations of the above choices, we are looking at 8 possible forms. The following sections cover each form in turn, showing how the form is constructed for each verb group, giving examples.

1.1 Dictionary Form

The plain, non-negative, non-past form of a verb may be known as its dictionary form. It is in this form that we will give verbs in the dictionary, and it is also this form that a hint for a verb will display before displaying the verbs true form (for a given sentence).
