Grammar Point 1: The passive form

1.1 The passive form

In the sentences that we have studied up until now, the basic pattern has been that the subject is the actor who performs the action described by the verb. In the passive construction, the focus is shifted so that the subject is the person or thing acted upon. You can see the difference between these two types of sentences by comparing how they describe the same events.

1.2 Expressing respect with the passive form

The passive form of a verb can also be used to show respect when describing the actions of a person of higher social status. This type of sentence is somewhat politer than normal speech but not as polite as the honorific 'keego' forms that we will study later. Unlike other sentences with the passive form, in these type of sentences the subject remains the actor and only the verb is changed to the passive form. Thus, just like a regular sentence the subject marked with ‚Í or ‚ª is the actor and the direct object marked with ‚ğ is acted upon by the subject.

Grammar Point 2: The causative passive form

Recall that the causative construction that we studied in the last lesson is used to express that someone is allowed to or made to do an action. This construction can be combined with the use of the passive in section 1.3 to show that someone was forced to do something and did not enjoy it or that the speaker empathises with the subject who was forced to do something. Though this type of sentence may appear confusing at first, you can make sense of it if you take a look at the words that compose it. The subject, in other words the person who is made or forced to do the action, is marked with ‚Í or ‚ª . The actor, who forces the subject to do something, is marked with ‚É . Direct objects, which are acted on by the subject, are marked by ‚ğ . The verb shows the action that the subject was forced to do, and is in the causative passive form.
