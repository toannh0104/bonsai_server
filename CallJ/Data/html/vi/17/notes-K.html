The sentences you create in this lesson should all be similar to the forms shown above. The grammar required to form these sentences is covered in the text below.



Grammar Point 1: Recommendations and preferences

1.1 Recommendations

First, we will look at an expression used to give advice or recommendations to others. This expression corresponds to the phrase "You should ..." in English, and is a polite way to recommend that someone should do something. Study the following examples.

Examples:

明日京都に行った方がいいです - "You should go to Kyoto tomorrow."

英語を勉強した方がいいです - "You should study English."

In this expression, the plain past form of the verb that shows the action to be done is followed by "hou ga ii desu". For the plain form, the "desu" at the end of the sentence can be dropped.

1.2 Preferences

The expression we looked at in the last section can actually be used to show a preference for one thing when comparing two or more things. This expression is used when the speaker has a choice between two or more alternatives. In this case, instead of the plain past form of a verb, we use a noun directly followed by the marker "no". Here are a few examples.

Examples:

ピザの方がいいです - "Pizza would be better."

この本の方がいいです - "This book is better."

Grammar Point 2: Expressing obligations

To express that someone must do something, the following expression is used. It corresponds to the expressions "must" or "have to" in English, and is used to describe both external obligations placed on the subject by someone else or the speaker's convictions about what needs to be done. To form this expression, change the plain negative form of the verb into the "ba" form and add the negative form of the verb "naru".

Examples:

宿題を出さなければならない - "You have to turn in your homework." (plain)

今日は早く帰らなければなりません - "I must go home early today." (polite)

2.1 The negative "ba" form of verbs

The negative "ba" form of verbs is used in the expression above, but it can also be used to make conditional statements like the normal "ba" form. This kind of sentence describes the result that occurs if the action in the conditional clause is not performed. To construct this form, the final "i" sound of the plain negative form is replaced with "kereba". See the examples below.

1Recommendations
はやくかえったほうがいいYou should go home early.
わたしをあまりおこらせないほうがいいYou shouldn't make me so angry.
そちらのけいかくのほうがいいですThat plan is better.
2Obligations
がっこうにいかなければならないI have to go to school.
べんきょうをしなければならないI have to study.
おとこにはやらなければならないことがあるA man has to do what a man has to do.
3Permission
あすいったらいいですYou can just go tomorrow.
そうしたらいいですYou can do that.
ぜんぶあなたのおもうようにしたらいいですYou can do it all however you please.

Examples:

切符を買わなければ電車に乗れません - "If you don't buy a ticket, you can't ride the train."

明日雨が降らなければ行けます - "It it doesn't rain tomorrow, I can go."

Grammar Point 3: Giving permission and making suggestions

Finally, we will look at another expression used to give permission. This expression gives permission to do the specified action and suggests that it will be enough for the situation. It carries the nuance that other choices are also acceptable. It is similar to the expressions "You can just..." or "It will be fine if you..." in English.

Examples:

先生に聞いたらいいです - "Just ask the professor."

スーツを着たらいいです - "You can just wear a suit."

Remember that a sentence that uses the "te" form of the verb followed by "mo ii desu" gives permission to do the specified action. In this case, the focus is on the permission only. In contrast, a sentence using the "tara" form of a verb followed by "ii desu" is more of a suggestion that implies permission. This expression is often used when giving advice or recommending a course of action.
