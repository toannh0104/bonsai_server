The sentence you create in this lesson should all be similar to the forms shown above. The grammar required to form these sentences is covered in the text below.



Grammar Point 1: Reasons with から

から is used to connect a subordinate clause to a main clause, and indicates that the subordinate clause is the reason for the main one. In this context, you can think of both clauses as simple sentences which could be said on their own. から plays the same role as English expressions such as 'since,' 'so,' or 'because.' Unlike English, the subordinate clause with the reason always comes first in Japanese. Study the following examples.

風邪を引いたから、今日は休んだ。"I took the day off because I caught a cold."
明日は土曜日だから、学校に行かなくてもいい。"Because tomorrow is Saturday, you don't have to go to school."
暑いから窓を開けよう。"Since it's hot, let's open a window."

Subordinate clauses that precede から , like other kinds of subordinate clauses, are in the plain form. This is true even when the main clause is in the polite form. However, in formal situations both the subordinate and main clause may be changed into the polite form.

In addition to descriptive statements, the main clause can also be a suggestion or request.

明日は休みだから、映画を見に行きませんか。"Because we have the day off tomorrow, why don't we go to see a movie?"
財布を忘れたから、千円を貸してもらえない？"Since I forgot my wallet, could you lend me 1000 yen?"

Grammar Point 2: Reasons with ので

ので functions almost identically to から , but it has some additional restrictions on when it can be used. First, the reason in the subordinate clause must be convincing to the listener as well as the speaker. Though から can be used then the subordinate clause is the speaker's conjecture or when the main clause is a request, suggestion, or invitation, ので cannot be used in these cases. For these reasons, ので has a politer, more objective nuance while から is more subjective.

日本に住んでいるので、いつでもお寿司が食べられます。"Since I live in Japan, I can eat sushi anytime."
大阪は遠いのであまり行きません。"Because it's far, I don't go to Osaka often."

Note that when the subordinate clause ends with the copula だ or a "na" adjective, the final だ changes to な. Study the following examples.

締め切りは明日なので、時間があまりありません。"Because the deadline is tomorrow, I don't have much time."
図書館は静かなので、そこで勉強する学生が多い。"Since the library is quiet, many students study there."

Grammar Point 3: Explanations with んだ or のです

This grammar pattern can be used to give an explanation for something that both the speaker and the listener know about. Often this shared information is something that both have directly observed or heard. When the copula だ or a "na" adjective is used the final だ changes to な , in the same way as subordinate clauses with ので .

昨日の試験は難しかったのです。"Yesterday's test was difficult."
田中さんはお酒を飲まないのだ。"Mr. Tanaka doesn't drink alcohol."
期末試験は大変なのです。"The final exam is tough."
あきこさんはとてもきれいなのだ。"Akiko is really pretty."

This grammar pattern can also be used when no information is shared between the speaker and the listener, or in situations other than explanations. In this case, the speaker uses this grammar pattern to emphasise a point and involve the listener in the conversation as if information was shared between them (as in the first examples).

この本はとても面白いのです。"This book is very interesting."
明日試験があるのだ。"I have an exam tomorrow."

In conversation and informal settings, のです is frequently shortened to んです . The informal version のだ is also shortened in these situations, but male and female speakers use different forms. Male speakers use んだ and female speakers use の . Study the following examples.

来週は東京に行くんだ。"I'm going to Tokyo next week." (male)
来週は東京に行くの。"I'm going to Tokyo next week." (female)
去年は京都に住んでいたんです。"I lived in Kyoto last year."
期末試験は簡単なんだ。"The final exam is easy." (male)
期末試験は簡単なの。"The final exam is easy." (female)
ジョンはイギリス人なんです。"John is British."
